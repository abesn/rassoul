The Arabic word *rassoul* (رسول) means "messenger" or "envoy" — one who is sent with a message. It comes from the trilateral root **R-S-L** (ر-س-ل), which carries the sense of dispatching, sending forth, and letting something proceed with ease.

## The Root R-S-L and Its Core Meaning

Classical Arabic lexicons such as *Lisān al-ʿArab* trace the root **ر-س-ل** to a base meaning of *irsāl* — sending, dispatching, or releasing. From this root come a family of related words: *arsala* (he sent), *irsāl* (the act of sending), *risālah* (a message or letter), *mursil* (sender), *mursal* (one who is sent), and *rasūl* (a messenger).

The root also carries a secondary sense of gentleness and unhurried flow. A camel walking with a steady, easy gait is described with a word from this same root. This nuance is not incidental: a *rasūl* is not merely one who is thrown forward, but one who is sent with purpose, carrying something entrusted to him, moving with dignity toward a destination.

So when we say <Arabic>{"رسول الله"}</Arabic> — *Rasūlullāh* — we are naming a person who has been sent by Allah, bearing a message from Him, and who delivers that message faithfully.

## Rasūl in the Qur'an

The Qur'an uses the word *rasūl* and its derivatives hundreds of times. It is applied to prophets, to angels who carry divine commands, and even to human envoys sent between people. The word appears in some of the most foundational verses of the faith, including the second half of the *shahādah* itself: <Arabic>{"مُحَمَّدٌ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ"}</Arabic> — *Muḥammadun Rasūlullāh* — "Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah."

The Qur'an also uses the plural *rusul* (رسل) to speak of the long chain of messengers sent to every nation, and *mursalīn* (مرسلين) — "those who are sent." Each of these words is a branch of the same root, and each reinforces the same idea: guidance did not arise from human speculation but was delivered by chosen envoys.

## Rasūl vs. Nabī: A Subtle Distinction

Arabic has two closely related terms that English often collapses into one: *nabī* (نبي, prophet) and *rasūl* (رسول, messenger). While scholars have discussed the distinction for centuries, the majority position is that every *rasūl* is a *nabī*, but not every *nabī* is a *rasūl*.

A *nabī* is one who receives revelation (*waḥy*) from Allah. A *rasūl* is a *nabī* who is additionally charged with conveying a new message, a new law (*sharīʿah*), or a specific mission to a people. The word *rasūl* therefore carries an extra weight: the burden of public delivery, of standing before a community, of being answerable for the transmission of a trust.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is described in the Qur'an with both titles — *al-Nabī* and *al-Rasūl* — and often together as <Arabic>{"النبي الرسول"}</Arabic>, indicating that in him both roles reached their fullness and their seal.

## Why the Domain Name "Rassoul"

The spelling *rassoul* is a French-influenced transliteration of the same Arabic word *rasūl* (رسول). English-speakers more often render it as *rasool*, *rasul*, or *rasūl*, but the underlying word is identical. All of these are attempts to capture in Latin letters what Arabic writes with four letters: <Arabic>{"ر س و ل"}</Arabic> — *rā, sīn, wāw, lām*.

The doubled "ss" in the French style reflects a convention for keeping the *sīn* sound crisp (rather than softening to a "z"), and the "ou" reflects the long *ū* vowel. So *rassoul*, *rasool*, and *rasūl* are three doors into one word.

For a site oriented around da'wah, the name signals its subject at once: the one sent by Allah, the message he carried, and the responsibility of every believer to know him and love him.

## The Semantic Family of R-S-L

Understanding the root helps unlock a whole vocabulary you will encounter throughout Islamic texts:

- **Risālah (رسالة)** — a message, a letter, or a mission. The overall message of a prophet is his *risālah*.
- **Arsala (أرسل)** — the verb "to send." Allah *arsala* His messengers.
- **Mursal (مُرسَل)** — one who has been sent; a passive participle. The plural *mursalīn* is used repeatedly in the Qur'an.
- **Mursil (مُرسِل)** — the sender. Allah is *al-Mursil* of every rasūl.
- **Tarassul (ترسّل)** — to proceed calmly, without haste, drawing on the root's secondary sense of gentleness.

This constellation of words shapes how Muslims speak about revelation. Prophecy is not framed as human ascent but as divine dispatch. The messenger is not the origin of the message; he is its trusted bearer.

## What This Means for a Muslim's Relationship with the Prophet ﷺ

Recognising Muhammad ﷺ as *Rasūlullāh* is not simply a linguistic label. It carries three practical consequences.

First, it means his message is not his own. He was sent, and what he conveyed traces back to the One who sent him. To reject the messenger is to reject the sender.

Second, it means his authority in matters of religion is binding. A messenger who is not obeyed has been sent in vain, and Allah does not send in vain. Obedience to the Rasūl is treated in the Qur'an as inseparable from obedience to Allah.

Third, it means his character was itself part of the message. A rasūl does not merely deliver words; he embodies them. The Prophet ﷺ walked with the gentleness that the root R-S-L itself hints at — a steady, purposeful, dignified bearing that his companions could imitate.

To say *Muḥammadun Rasūlullāh*, then, is to affirm all of this in a single phrase: that he was sent, that his message is trustworthy, that his authority is real, and that his life is a pattern.
