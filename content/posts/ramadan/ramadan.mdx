The month of Ramadan is the sacred period in which the Qur’an was revealed, making it a time of obligatory fasting for every able Muslim, as decreed by Allah for our spiritual growth and gratitude.

## The Divine Decree of Fasting

The obligation to fast during Ramadan is established directly in the Qur’an. Allah states clearly:

<Arabic>{\"شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أُنزِلَ فِيهِ ٱلْقُرْءَانُ هُدًى لِّلنَّاسِ وَبَيِّنَـٰتٍ مِّنَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ وَٱلْفُرْقَانِ ۚ فَمَن شَهِدَ مِنكُمُ ٱلشَّهْرَ فَلْيَصُمْهُ ۖ وَمَن كَانَ مَرِيضًا أَوْ عَلَىٰ سَفَرٍ فَعِدَّةٌ مِّنْ أَيَّامٍ أُخَرَ ۗ يُرِيدُ ٱللَّهُ بِكُمُ ٱلْيُسْرَ وَلَا يُرِيدُ بِكُمُ ٱلْعُسْرَ وَلِتُكْمِلُوا۟ ٱلْعِدَّةَ وَلِتُكَبِّرُوا۟ ٱللَّهَ عَلَىٰ مَا هَدَىٰكُمْ وَلَعَلَّكُمْ تَشْكُرُونَ"}</Arabic>

*“The month of Ramadan in which was revealed the Qur’ân, a guidance for mankind and clear proofs for the guidance and the criterion (between right and wrong). So whoever of you sights (the crescent on the first night of) the month (of Ramadan i.e. is present at his home), he must observe Saum (fasts) that month, and whoever is ill or on a journey, the same number [of days which one did not observe Saum (fasts) must be made up] from other days. Allâh intends for you ease, and He does not want to make things difficult for you. (He wants that you) must complete the same number (of days), and that you must magnify Allâh [i.e. to say Takbîr (Allâhu Akbar; Allâh is the Most Great] for having guided you so that you may be grateful to Him.”* <Citation source="Quran" book="2" number="185" href="https://quran.com/2/185" />

This verse highlights several key aspects: Ramadan is the month when the Qur’an was sent down as a guide. Fasting is mandatory for those who are present and healthy. Exceptions are made for the ill and travelers, who must make up the days later. The underlying purpose is ease, not hardship, and the completion of the prescribed number of days leads to gratitude.

## The Blessed Night

The revelation of the Qur’an began during a specific night within Ramadan. Allah refers to this night in another verse:

<Arabic>{"إِنَّآ أَنزَلْنَـٰهُ فِى لَيْلَةٍ مُّبَـٰرَكَةٍ ۚ إِنَّا كُنَّا مُنذِرِينَ"}</Arabic>

*“We sent it (this Qur’ân) down on a blessed night [(i.e. night of Al-Qadr, Sûrah No. 97) in the month of Ramadân - the 9th month of the Islâmic calendar]. Verily, We are ever warning [mankind that Our Torment will reach those who disbelieve in Our Oneness of Lordship and in Our Oneness of worship].”* <Citation source="Quran" book="44" number="3" href="https://quran.com/44/3" />

This night, known as Laylat al-Qadr (the Night of Decree), is described as “blessed” and marks the commencement of the Qur’anic revelation. While not specified in this verse alone, scholars identify it with the latter part of Ramadan, as further detailed in other chapters (such as Surah al-Qadr, 97). Muslims seek this night for its immense spiritual reward.

## The Purpose of Ramadan

Fasting in Ramadan is not merely abstaining from food and drink. It is a comprehensive act of worship that cultivates taqwa (God-consciousness), self-discipline, and empathy for the less fortunate. The verse from Surah al-Baqarah emphasizes that the goal is to magnify Allah and express gratitude. By controlling physical desires during daylight hours, a Muslim strengthens their connection to the Creator and becomes more mindful of His commands.

## Actions and Etiquettes

While the provided sources do not include specific hadiths on Ramadan practices, the Qur’anic verses alone guide core actions:

- **Observing the moon sighting** to determine the start and end of Ramadan, as implied by “whoever of you sights (the crescent on the first night of) the month.”
- **Fulfilling the fast** from dawn to sunset for the entire month.
- **Making up missed days** due to illness or travel.
- **Reciting and reflecting on the Qur’an**, given that it was revealed during this month.
- **Increasing in supplication and gratitude**, particularly by saying “Allahu Akbar” as instructed.

## Exemptions and Mercy

Allah’s mercy is evident in the allowances provided. The verse explicitly states: *“Allâh intends for you ease, and He does not want to make things difficult for you.”* This is a fundamental principle of Islamic law. Those who are ill or traveling are not burdened with fasting during those conditions but must compensate later. Similarly, those who are permanently unable to fast (e.g., due to chronic illness or old age) have other options, such as feeding the poor—a provision detailed elsewhere in the Qur’an (2:184).

## Conclusion

Ramadan is a divinely appointed month of revelation, fasting, and spiritual renewal. The Qur’an establishes its obligatory nature, its purpose of guidance and gratitude, and the mercy embedded within its rulings. Every Muslim is encouraged to embrace this month wholeheartedly, seeking closeness to Allah through fasting, prayer, and recitation of the Qur’an.
