Ramadan 2026 is expected to begin on the evening of Tuesday, February 17, 2026, and conclude around Wednesday, March 18, 2026, subject to the sighting of the crescent moon in your local region. As always, the confirmed start and end dates depend on moon sighting announcements from local scholars and authorities.

## When Does Ramadan 2026 Begin

Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar, and its arrival is determined by the sighting of the new crescent (hilāl). Astronomical calculations suggest that 1 Ramadan 1447 AH will correspond to Wednesday, February 18, 2026, meaning the first night of tarāwīh and the pre-dawn suḥūr for the first fast will fall on the evening of Tuesday, February 17.

Because the Islamic calendar is roughly eleven days shorter than the Gregorian one, Ramadan 2026 will arrive earlier than Ramadan 2025. Muslims in the Northern Hemisphere can expect fasts of moderate length, with daylight hours gradually lengthening across the month as winter transitions into early spring.

It is worth remembering that a difference of a day between countries is normal and rooted in the Sunnah itself. Communities have historically relied on their own local sighting or that of a trusted authority nearby. Rather than being a cause for dispute, this variation reflects the mercy and flexibility built into the sharī'ah.

## Why Ramadan Holds Such a High Station

Allah singled out Ramadan among all months by choosing it as the vessel for His final revelation. He says:

<Arabic>{"شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ ٱلَّذِىٓ أُنزِلَ فِيهِ ٱلْقُرْءَانُ هُدًى لِّلنَّاسِ وَبَيِّنَـٰتٍ مِّنَ ٱلْهُدَىٰ وَٱلْفُرْقَانِ"}</Arabic>

*Shahru Ramaḍāna alladhī unzila fīhi al-Qur'ānu hudan lin-nāsi wa bayyinātin min al-hudā wal-furqān.*

"The month of Ramadan in which was revealed the Qur'an, a guidance for mankind and clear proofs for the guidance and the criterion." <Citation source="Quran" book="Al-Baqarah" number="2:185" href="https://quran.com/2/185" />

The same verse then establishes the obligation of fasting for those who witness the month, while permitting the ill and the traveller to make up missed days later. The verse closes with a striking reminder of divine intention: Allah wants ease for His servants, not hardship, and He wants us to complete the count and magnify Him in gratitude for guidance.

Allah also describes the night in which the Qur'an was sent down as a blessed night:

<Arabic>{"إِنَّآ أَنزَلْنَـٰهُ فِى لَيْلَةٍ مُّبَـٰرَكَةٍ ۚ إِنَّا كُنَّا مُنذِرِينَ"}</Arabic>

*Innā anzalnāhu fī laylatin mubārakah, innā kunnā mundhirīn.*

"We sent it down on a blessed night. Verily, We are ever warning." <Citation source="Quran" book="Ad-Dukhan" number="44:3" href="https://quran.com/44/3" />

This blessed night, understood by the majority of scholars to be Laylat al-Qadr within the last ten nights of Ramadan, gives the month a weight unmatched by any other on the calendar.

## Preparing Spiritually Before Ramadan 2026 Arrives

The companions and early generations began preparing for Ramadan months in advance. A common practice among the righteous predecessors was to ask Allah for six months to reach Ramadan, and then to ask Him for six months afterwards to accept what they had offered.

Practical preparation before Ramadan 2026 begins can include:

- **Making up missed fasts** from previous years, if any remain, before the new Ramadan arrives.
- **Strengthening voluntary fasts** in Rajab and Sha'bān so the body and soul are already acclimated.
- **Reviewing your relationship with the Qur'an**, since Ramadan is fundamentally the month of the Qur'an. Setting a realistic daily portion now prevents scrambling later.
- **Settling debts and disputes**, so the heart enters the month unburdened.
- **Reducing distractions**, particularly the low-value screen habits that quietly consume the hours meant for worship.

The goal is not perfection on day one, but momentum. A believer who enters Ramadan already in motion is far more likely to make the most of its final ten nights.

## Fasting: The Core Act of Worship

Fasting in Ramadan is one of the five pillars of Islam. From the true dawn (fajr) until sunset (maghrib), the fasting person abstains from food, drink, and marital intimacy, alongside the moral discipline of guarding the tongue, the eyes, and the temper.

Allah's own words in Al-Baqarah frame this obligation with mercy: whoever is ill or on a journey may defer the fast and make up the same number of days later. Pregnancy, nursing, chronic illness, and old age each have their own rulings, and consulting a qualified scholar for one's specific circumstances is part of practising the religion correctly.

The daily rhythm of Ramadan is anchored by two meals: **suḥūr** just before dawn and **ifṭār** at sunset. Both carry blessing. Suḥūr fortifies the body for the day ahead, while ifṭār is a moment of quiet gratitude, traditionally opened with dates and water before the maghrib prayer.

## The Nights: Tarāwīh and the Last Ten

The nights of Ramadan carry their own distinct worship. After 'ishā', Muslims gather for **tarāwīh**, the long congregational prayer in which the imam typically recites a portion of the Qur'an such that the entire text is completed by the end of the month.

The final ten nights are the crown of the month. It is within them that Laylat al-Qadr is sought, the night described by Allah as better than a thousand months. Many Muslims observe **i'tikāf**, secluding themselves in the mosque to devote these nights to prayer, Qur'an, and remembrance. Even those unable to perform full i'tikāf can carve out extended nightly worship, especially on the odd nights: the 21st, 23rd, 25th, 27th, and 29th.

## Zakāt al-Fitr and the End of the Month

Before the 'Eid prayer that marks the end of Ramadan, every Muslim who has surplus food for the day and night of 'Eid must pay **Zakāt al-Fitr** on behalf of themselves and their dependents. It is a small, fixed amount of staple food (or its monetary equivalent, according to the position of one's scholars) given to the poor so that they too can share in the joy of 'Eid.

Ramadan 2026 will conclude with the sighting of the Shawwāl crescent, expected around Wednesday, March 18, 2026, followed by 'Eid al-Fiṭr the next morning. The verse of fasting itself directs us toward this closing note: to magnify Allah for having guided us, and to be among the grateful.

May Allah allow us to witness Ramadan 2026 in a state of health and faith, to fast its days and stand its nights sincerely, and to accept from us what we offer.
